The playbook

What he actually did

~40 tactics across product, branding, creators, content, paid media, ops, mindset and hiring. Full write-up in INSIGHTS.md.

🛍️

Product strategy

  • "Saturation is king." A product everyone sells with no dominant brand is market-validated demand waiting to be owned.
  • Watch for unusual longevity. Most of his 180 SKUs lasted ~2 months; the slipper sold 12 months straight — that persistence flagged it as brandable.
  • Borrow adjacent blueprints. He mapped The Oodie's "designs + licensing" model onto slippers.
  • Define avatars by reason-to-buy, not demographics (pain-relief / travel / at-home).
  • If starting over: pick e-com-friendly products — 1 SKU, low weight, subscription potential.
✨

Branding & positioning

  • Authority transfer beats features. The right people using it made a "boring" product cool faster than product tweaks.
  • "Act like a billion-dollar company" — real shoots, A-list creators, IP capsules: signature moves rivals won't match.
  • Emotion > function as you scale — from "ergonomic slipper" to selling happiness.
  • Reposition near the ceiling — he reframed "footwear" into "fashion" to unlock new creators and pricing.
  • Manufacture "the original." By branding hard first, the market believed his was the original.
📣

Creators — find & test

  • Stories convert, not reels/posts. "Stories is king" — that's where purchase intent lives.
  • Buy 2-week story rights, run as ads in your own Meta account, then scale-or-kill in 2-week blocks.
  • Whitelist small/mid creators (native trust); big celebs often refuse — run both.
  • Hunt at volume. ~200 tested → ~20 winners (~10%). Expect a low hit rate; it's a portfolio game.
  • Investigate unexplained revenue — he phoned Shopify customers to find his first A-player.
  • Judge on conversion, not clout — "not engagement rate or follower likes… completely different."
🤝

Creators — keep & scale winners

  • Hoard winners. "I found gold — take care of it." Go meet them; make them loyal.
  • Treat them as partners, not vendors — dinners, visits, early access, bonuses.
  • Relationship = moat. A rival can't poach an A-player with a slightly higher CPM if the bond is real.
  • Build a creator CRM — track revenue, CPA, geography, avatar fit, content type.
  • Use their content as your paid engine — every deal is a content deal first.
🎬

Content & creative

  • Content must be avatar-deep — creators must know each avatar's pains, routines, language.
  • Bring content in-house at scale — he acquired a content agency; outside shops juggling 100 clients can't keep pace.
  • One product, three messages — customize the angle per avatar.
  • License IP for instant cool — Snoopy, Harry Potter, Coca-Cola, Warner Bros (long approval cycles).
📊

Paid media & omnichannel

  • Be omnipresent by default, optimize later — "do more": more channels, countries, creatives.
  • Creator content = refreshable ad supply via the 2-week usage pattern; judge by CPA/ROAS, not likes.
  • Google for both TOF & retargeting — don't over-theorize which channel "does what"; let data split budget.
⚙️

Scaling & operations

  • Don't 10× in a month unless ops are bulletproof — his $10k→$100k/day jump crashed logistics.
  • Grow stably, serving customers — gate ad spend behind inventory, 3PL, CS and payments readiness.
  • "Serve, don't just sell." Quality and delivery are the brand; failing them is failing to serve.
🧠

Mindset & leadership

  • Decide fast, adapt in motion — "I never know, I just decide… do the thinking during the process."
  • Don't decide from your own taste — "it's the customer, 100% the customer."
  • Fail fast — "fail is not even a word, it's a lesson."
  • Protect energy — weekends are sacred and near-unreachable.
  • Humility as anti-fragility — ego after early riches nearly cost him everything.
  • Master acquisition first, then layer LTV.
👥

Hiring

  • Solve one "gap of the year" with one transformational hire (content → branding → product dev).
  • Hire by personal goals, not job specs — connect the role to the person's life ambition to win loyalty money can't buy.
🔎

Attribution, launch & expansion

  • Call customers when attribution is murky — manual source-of-truth beats a confused dashboard.
  • Use 2-week windows as a creative sprint cadence — brief, shoot, test, renew winners, discard losers.
  • Enter countries via the right creators, not just translations — geo-specific shortlists by avatar.
  • Plan one "category-bending" move per quarter (top creator / IP drop / editorial film).
Deep dives

The nuance behind the soundbites

The parts worth slowing down on — expand each.

The 2-week rights test, explained 40:20

He doesn't buy the post — he buys the usage rights to a creator's story content for 2 weeks, runs it as a paid ad on his own Meta account, and reads the numbers. Works → renew the rights for another 2 weeks and scale spend. Doesn't → kill it.

Two weeks is deliberate: long enough for a clean read, short enough to dodge creative fatigue and keep licensing cheap and low-commitment. It's standard ad-creative testing, applied to licensed creator content instead of in-house creative.

Why obsess over a winning creator 38:28

Winners are rare (10% hit rate) and poachable. The asset isn't the one viral story — it's the ongoing relationship. Dinners, visits and real loyalty buy exclusivity (they won't run a competitor), first access, better rates, higher-quality content, and a true ambassador. In a category anyone can copy, your roster of loyal A-creators is the part rivals can't clone.

Whitelisting — what it is and why "run both" 41:40

Whitelisting = running ads through the creator's own handle, so the ad appears to come from a real person — their followers, history and face — not your brand page. That native social proof converts better than a brand-account ad.

Small/mid creators agree easily; big celebrities resist handing over account access (control, brand-safety, exclusivity). So the play is a portfolio: whitelist the small ones for cheap native trust, run brand-account or spark ads for the big names.

The accidental discovery — the repeatable lesson 43:41

Revenue doubled with no explanation, so he didn't trust the dashboard — he phoned Shopify customers and asked "where did you buy this?" That surfaced a creator who'd sold ~$10k with zero contact. The principles: (1) when a number moves and you can't explain it, hunt the source by hand; (2) when you find gold, act at founder-speed and in person — he flew to her country, paid her, and she did $60k in a day. Most people would have sent a DM.

"12 months straight vs ~2" — what that tell means 05:50

In his 180-SKU store a typical product had a commercial lifespan of ~2 months before sales died (trend fades, ad fatigue). This slipper kept selling steadily for a full year — ~6× the norm. That durability — not a spike — signaled demand was structural, not faddish, i.e. worth branding into a real company.

The Oodie → licensing playbook 08:42

Davie Fogarty's The Oodie took a plain hoodie, added fun/licensed designs, and built a ~$550M brand. Santiago is doing the identical move on a commodity slipper — bolting on licensed IP (Snoopy, Harry Potter, Coca-Cola, Warner Bros, Batman, Superman).

Why it works: instant differentiation in a saturated category, borrowed brand equity + built-in fandom, collectibility (many designs = repeat purchase), premium pricing, and each license is its own campaign. The catch: IP deals take years and require you to prove your brand is legit first.

"I just decide" vs "don't trust your taste" — the apparent contradiction 1:06:23

Two different decisions. "I never know, I just decide" is for reversible, speed-sensitive choices — bias to action; you learn faster by moving (the general's story). "Don't decide from your own taste" is for judging what the market wants — the products he loved flopped, the ones he hated sold.

Synthesis: make the decision fast, but let the customer — not your ego — decide whether it was right. Action is yours; the verdict is the market's.

The three customer avatars 33:15

His slipper avatars: (1) ergonomic / pain-relief (nurses, people on their feet — the product is EVA compression material), (2) vacation / travel, (3) relax-at-home. One product, three emotionally distinct messages. "Good content" means the creator knows each avatar's pain, desire and lifestyle so deeply that the message lands as a feeling — which is exactly why he won't use a generic agency.

🔑 The Hidden Method

He says his creator-vetting is "completely different" from engagement/followers — but never reveals it. The system that fits every clue: story-first, down-funnel performance scoring — treat creators as creative suppliers for paid, judged by cost-per-acquisition, not clout.

1

Pre-qualify on fit + story behavior

Audience–avatar match, story views per follower, link-tap rate, replies per 1,000 views (intimacy > likes).

2

Price per story view (CPSV)

Quote ÷ average story views. Model expected CPA before booking — e.g. 100k views × 1% taps × 2% CVR = 20 orders.

3

Test their content in YOUR ad account

Mandatory 2-week paid usage rights; judge by CPA/ROAS and first-frame hold-rate in days 3–5. Kill fast, renew winners.

4

Whitelist intensification

For small/mid creators, compare brand-handle vs whitelisted-handle CPA — keep the cheaper.

5

Confirm attribution manually

Unique codes + post-purchase survey + phone calls when the dashboard is murky.

6

Manage the portfolio

Per-creator rolling P&L and geo benchmarks — the "I know exactly which influencers in France" advantage.

The big idea: stop shopping for reach. Pre-qualify by story-consumption quality + avatar fit, set a price-per-view you can make work, then let down-funnel paid performance decide. That's performance media buying — with creators as the creative source.

Before you copy it

Pressure-test the playbook

It's one founder's path, not a law of nature. Validate these in your own business first.

"Stories only" is brand-specific. Plenty of brands sell hard on reels/TikTok. Test formats in your category before going stories-only.
The 80/20 is a power law. A handful of creators likely drive most of it — expect concentration, and don't over-learn from one lucky early hit.
"Act like a billion-dollar company" needs unit economics first. Big shoots and celebrity collabs are leverage after your CAC/LTV pencil — not before.
Gate spend behind ops readiness. His 10× month crashed logistics. Put kill-switches on spend and inventory risk before you scale.
Acquisition-first vs LTV depends on the product. Great for a one-off purchase; a subscription brand should build retention much earlier.
Verify with your own data. AOV, gross margin, return rate, and CPA by channel decide whether any of this works for you.
